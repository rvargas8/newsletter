Ben Prints Something Important

Historical Fiction - Philadelphia, 1776

Twelve-year-old Ben carefully picked up the small metal letters. He was learning to be a printer. Ben worked in a printing shop owned by Mr. Hall. Ben's job was to arrange the metal letters to make words. It was hard work because he had to put the letters backward! That way, they would print the right way on paper.

18

Today was special. A tall man with red hair had brought an important paper to the shop. His name was Mr. Jefferson. Mr. Hall told Ben to be very careful. "This paper is a declaration of independence," Mr. Hall said. "It says America wants to be free from the King of England."

28

Ben's hands shook a little as he worked. He knew this was dangerous. The King might punish people for printing this paper. But Ben kept working. He read the words as he set them: "All men are created equal."

36

Ben's friend Jack whispered, "My father says this might start a war. Are you scared?"

40

Ben thought for a moment. "Maybe a little," he said. "But these words say people should be free. That's important, right?"

46

After hours of work, Ben finished arranging all the letters. The adult workers put ink on the letters. Then they used the big wooden press to print the words onto paper. Pull! Press! The machine made each copy.

54

Ben watched the first printed papers come out. The ink was still wet. These papers would be sent all over the colonies. People would read them in every town.

62

Ben felt scared and excited at the same time. He was just a printer's helper, but he had helped make something that could change the world. His hands had set the words that told everyone America wanted to be free.
